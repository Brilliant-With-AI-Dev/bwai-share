Plain English overview

What is this? Scope it. Staff it. Watch it run. You say what you need — "a board update," "a hiring memo" — and an AI scopes it with you, staffs a small team of AI workers, and runs them like a relay until a finished document lands. It's a live demo of our platform, and what you watch is real.

Who is it for? Busy managers and leaders — nontechnical. The demo is meant to be watched and understood without an engineering background.

What you get? A real, finished deliverable — produced by genuine platform calls, not a canned animation. The AI workers don't exist until the moment each is needed; nothing is staged in advance.

Today vs. After vs. Why it matters

Today

You re-explain who you are and what you want on every new piece of work. AI tools forget you between sessions.

After

The demo remembers three things — who you are, what happened before, and what's happening right now — so you stop repeating yourself and the work picks up where it left off.

Why it matters

Less re-explaining, faster setup, and recurring work (a weekly update) that genuinely remembers last period.

TL;DR (for the technically curious)

  • Single-file demo: a Node server boots the live platform in-process and drives it through four steps with real permissions.
  • Three memory layers map to platform context-asset scopes: identity profile (user/org scope), workflow memory (custom workflow scope), within-run relay (task scope).
  • A demo gate (three named sign-ins over one shared account) segments each person's profile, workflows, and memory. It's a curtain for demo hygiene, not production auth.
  • An in-app docent answers questions about the demo — itself a platform chat call, grounded in a written doc plus a live snapshot of the current user's state.
  • The run's live state is in-memory and wiped on restart. Saved workflows and durable memory persist through the platform.

The step-by-step flow

A "deliverable" is the finished thing you asked for — a document, a plan, a draft. The demo moves through four visible steps:

  1. Scope. You describe what you want. The AI proposes a clear objective and a few alternative framings, so you pick the one you mean before any work starts.
  2. Discovery. A second AI gathers the details it needs — but only asks what it can't already figure out from what you've told it. Fewer questions is better.
  3. Design the team. A third AI designs the smallest team of AI workers (usually two to four) that can produce the deliverable. Each worker gets a role and a goal.
  4. Run (the relay). Workers run in order, left to right. Each finishes its part and hands the next a brief — a short hand-off note. That brief is the baton. The relay ends in the finished deliverable. 
  Scope  ─▶  Discovery  ─▶  Design team  ─▶  Run (relay)  ─▶  Deliverable
                                            │
                          ┌─────────────────┴─────────────────┐
                       Worker 1  ──brief──▶  Worker 2  ──brief──▶  Worker 3
                       (gather)             (draft)              (polish)
                          the "brief" is the baton passed left → right

After the run, if the work recurs, you can save it as a reusable workflow and re-run it in two clicks.

What a "context asset" is (no jargon)

A context asset is a small saved piece of information the platform keeps and hands to the right AI step at the right moment — like a sticky note the system files away and pulls out exactly when a worker needs it.

The useful idea is scope: how widely a note applies and how long it sticks around. Three sizes:

  • This run only — matters for one job, thrown away after (the briefs workers pass).
  • This workflow — tied to a recurring piece of work, kept across every run (what happened last week).
  • This person / company — about you or your organization, kept across all your work (your role, company, audience).

You don't manage these notes yourself; the platform decides which to hand to which worker, when.

The three memory layers

This is the question managers ask most: "what does it remember about me?" Three distinct layers, each remembering different things.

Layer Scope Remembers When it's used
Identity profile You / your company WHO you are — name, role, company, industry, audience Answered once, saved by your choice; Discovery pre-fills or skips these basics afterward
Workflow memory This workflow WHAT happened — a rolling summary plus an append-only history of past runs Only for recurring saved workflows; opens the next run with a short "Previously…" recap
Within-run relay This run only The briefs and outputs workers hand each other During a single run; not carried forward

A quick way to hold all three: profile = who you are, workflow memory = what happened before, the relay = what's happening right now.

Honesty point A saved fact is reference material, not gospel. Pre-fills are labeled "from your profile" and are one keystroke to override. A confidently wrong pre-filled answer is worse than a blank one — if your role changes, update it. The recap is framed as history, possibly stale, not current truth. The system never saves silently; saving is always your explicit choice.

Personal facts (your name, title) are kept for you. Shared facts (company, industry, audience, voice) are kept for the whole organization, so a teammate's first build is already grounded.

Steps created on the fly — how it works

Real work is unpredictable — you can't list every step of a board memo up front. So this doesn't make you. It adds steps during the run, as it learns what the work actually needs.

How it works, in plain terms:

  1. It starts from your goal, not a fixed checklist.
  2. It does the first step — then looks at that result to decide the next one.
  3. If a step turns up something new (say, the draft needs a number checked), it creates that next step right then. It didn't have to know in advance.
  4. It keeps going, each step shaped by the one before, until the deliverable is done.

So the team isn't frozen the moment it's designed — it can grow a step whenever the work reveals one is needed.

Why it matters You discover what a deliverable needs as you go. A tool locked to a fixed plan either over-asks at the start or stalls when reality differs. Making steps on the fly is what lets it handle real, messy work instead of only what fits a template.

The demo gate (the sign-in)

The demo offers three named sign-ins so that during a live demo each teammate can sign in and see their own profile, their own saved workflows, and their own memory — as if they each had a separate account.

Be honest about what this is Under the hood it's all one shared demo account. The three names are a curtain, not real security — a convenience so people's demo content doesn't collide, not a protected login. There are basic measures so it isn't trivially bypassed (signed sign-in tokens, no passwords or keys in the code), but it is a demo convenience, never production-grade login.

The docent (in-app guide)

The page has a built-in guide — a chatbot you can ask "what does this remember about me?" or "is this real?" in plain language. It is itself a genuine platform chat call. It's grounded in a written explainer plus a live, privacy-safe snapshot of the current user's own state, so its answers match what you're actually looking at. It won't reveal credentials or internal details, and it admits when something is outside what it can see.

Real vs. demo-grade

On screen: "What you are watching is real, not a mock-up."

Real

  • Every step is a genuine call to the live platform, with real permissions — not a canned animation.
  • The AI workers are created on the fly, the moment each is needed. Nothing is staged in advance.
  • The deliverable produced is a real deliverable.

Demo-grade (staged)

  • The three-name sign-in gate — a curtain over one shared account.
  • The in-memory database — the live state of a run (which step is next, the team, the goal) is held in memory and wiped when the server restarts. Saved workflows and durable memory/profile records persist through the platform, but an in-progress run does not survive a restart, and you can't pause and resume a half-finished run.

If someone asks "is this actually doing the work or just showing me a movie?" — it's actually doing the work.
