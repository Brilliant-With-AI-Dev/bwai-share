The interview, working end-to-end, on an alternate stack.

A scripted walk through the mirror-next interview experience — from the opening question to the Insights Snapshot — built as a live sandbox so we can look at the interaction shape before we commit engineering to it.

What this is

A working sandbox of the mirror-next interview flow. Real chat scaffold, real streaming, real on-brand styling. The conversation is driven by a fixed script (mock LLM, hand-written replies) so the demo is deterministic and reviewable. The user composer is pre-populated each turn with the answer a real subject would give, then locked read-only — reviewers see the intended arc play out without typing a word.

Why we built it

Before we sink engineering hours into the rebuild, we wanted to see the interaction shape running in front of us. The questions we needed answers to:

  • Does the structured Q1–Q4 interview cadence feel right, or does it read as cold and mechanical?
  • Does a sidecar pane showing "working notes" as the interview progresses add signal, or just clutter?
  • Is the Insights Snapshot a strong-enough payoff to justify the wait, with the cuts landing the way the prompt intends?
  • What does the user actually do with the snapshot once it arrives — read, save, print, share?

The arc below is the answer. Skip to What this proves if you want the takeaways first.

Why assistant-ui — and what we're not locked into

The sandbox uses assistant-ui, an open-source React chat scaffold. We get the entire conversation surface — message stream, composer, dictation hook, accessibility, mobile behaviour — for free, branded against our own design tokens. What would have taken a day or two to hand-build took a handful of hours.

This is also a proof-of-concept of running on an alternate stack: the sandbox uses Vercel AI SDK + AI Gateway and makes zero calls to our current ThinkWAI platform. Adopting assistant-ui gets us shipping faster; replacing it later is a straightforward swap. Every UX concept here is framework-agnostic.

One thing to ignore in the screenshots. The small red "N · Issues" pill in the bottom-left of each frame is the Next.js dev-mode overlay — a developer tool, not part of the product. Skip past it whenever you see it.

The four beats

Click a thumbnail to jump to that step. Or use ← → to cycle through, Esc to collapse all.

Right pane: LISTENING Composer pre-filled, read-only No notes yet

The interviewer opens with a contextualised one-paragraph frame, sets a time expectation (~20 minutes), and asks Q1 — what you are accountable for this quarter and what would count as a win versus a problem. The voice is direct: no warmth, no throat-clearing, no AI-chat tics.

The right pane shows a soft "LISTENING" state with a pulse — signalling to the user that the interview is paying attention, and that observations will surface here as the conversation unfolds. The composer at the bottom is already filled in with the expected user reply (italic gray) and locked read-only, so the demo runs on a script and reviewers can see exactly what a real subject would say.

Right pane: WORKING NOTES · 1 → 2 Q2 then Q3 streamed Replies auto-prefilled

The interviewer asks Q2 (team shape) and then Q3 (AI in the wild). Each answer triggers a working note in the right pane — not a paraphrase of what the user said, but a sharper read of the pattern underneath it. The composer auto-rewrites between turns, keeping the demo on rails.

By the second note, the cumulative read is starting to look like a thesis. A stakeholder reading the transcript later can already see where the conversation is heading — without the interviewer ever breaking its neutral-question stance.

After Q2 — one note surfaces.

After Q3 — pattern accumulates.

Right pane: WORKING NOTES · 3 of 3 · ready Closing-question cue card Q4 streamed (CEO translation)

All three working notes are now visible. Below them, a soft accent-tinted cue strip reads "One last question, then the report drops" — telling the user the interview is wrapping up before they hear it from the interviewer itself. That parallel signalling is one of the things we wanted to test here: does the sidecar pane carry useful pacing information alongside the chat?

In the chat, Q4 is the closing question — what does the CEO actually want from AI in plain English, and how will they judge whether you delivered. The composer is pre-filled with the expected reply.

Right pane: INSIGHTS SNAPSHOT Copy · Download · Print TASK → ROLE → IDENTITY escalation

After the user's reply to Q4, the interviewer delivers a brief synthesis handoff in the chat — "I have what I need. Drafting your snapshot now." The right pane holds for a short drafting beat (about three seconds of "SYNTHESIZING"), then the Insights Snapshot streams in: a forensic, second-person read structured as a title, a thesis quote, three escalating insights (Task → Role → Identity), each ending in a bolded verdict sentence, and a two-line kicker callback.

The voice is the production mirror-next register: cold clarity, surgical, no reassurance. The snapshot is intentionally short enough to read in one sitting and sharp enough to be quotable. The Copy, Download, and Print buttons in the top-right of the pane are all client-only — no scaffold changes were needed. Download writes a timestamped markdown file; Print opens a browser print dialog that shows only the report.

In order of how much each matters for the next conversation:

  • The interaction shape works. The interview-plus-sidecar-plus-payoff structure reads as intentional and earns its real estate.
  • Brand and product feel intact on an alternate stack we don't fully control — no compromises in voice, typography, or pacing.
  • The alternate stack is viable, with no lock-in. The sandbox runs entirely on Vercel AI SDK + AI Gateway, with zero calls to the ThinkWAI platform. assistant-ui buys us velocity now; every concept here is framework-agnostic and swappable later.
  • Print / Copy / Download for the snapshot is essentially free because the artifact lives in our component, not in the scaffold's message stream.
