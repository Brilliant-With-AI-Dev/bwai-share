What we put head-to-head

One AI played the interviewer (each prompt in turn). A second AI played the same realistic person both times — "Matt, Head of Creative Operations at Wayfair," a calm VP quietly anxious about AI. Only the interviewer's prompt changed. A separate, different AI then read both transcripts and judged the quality of the conversation — how natural it felt, how well it flowed, how much it drew the person out, and how rich the material it produced.

Same moment, two interviewers

Both interviewers reached the same topics with the same person. Here are six moments side by side — Control (today's prompt) on the left, the simple prompt on the right, in their own words. Read them as conversation: which one feels alive, and which one gets the more revealing answer.

The opening line · first thing each interviewer said

Control · today's prompt

"Before I dig in, here's what I'm picking up: Your day job: you run the content factory… Your 'team' is a machine: a ~90-person mixed-discipline org… The AI ask is political now… Does that feel right?"

Simple prompt

"Creative ops is one of those worlds where 'AI' isn't abstract: it hits the throughput math, the on-brand guardrails, and the human pride of craft all at once, and suddenly you're responsible for all three. Where are your work hours actually going right now?"

One opened like a briefing; one opened like a conversation. Control led with a tidy summary of what it already knew and asked Matt to confirm it. The simple prompt named the human tension — pride of craft, "responsible for all three" — then handed him the floor. The first invites a yes/no; the second invites him to talk.

The Firefly retouch pilot · "what did it actually save?"

Control · today's prompt

"It does save minutes, but it also goes off-brand or introduces weird artifacts often enough that we've kept it very scoped… anything that creates even a 10% rework rate at our volumes is basically a non-starter."

Simple prompt

"Directionally the retouch step might drop from, say, 10–15 minutes down to 5–8 when it's a straightforward extension… The problem is the QA loop can go from essentially 'quick check' to 10+ minutes of scrutiny… on the good assets you net a few minutes, and on the bad ones you lose a lot more than you saved."

Richer answer. Both heard about the same pilot. Control got a clear qualitative read — "saves minutes," but "weird artifacts." The simple prompt's next question pressed for the actual before/after and surfaced the hidden trap: the time saved comes right back in QA. Same topic, more usable material.

After the pipeline walkthrough · what the interviewer said next

Control · today's prompt

[reflected the answer back] "So the pain isn't 'creative magic' — it's inputs, handoffs, and rework loops that eat the gains."

Simple prompt

[built on it, one level deeper] "On the metadata/ingestion break, what's the most common concrete error — wrong filename, missing attribute, wrong channel spec, version confusion — and how often does it happen (rough % of SKUs)?"

Reflect back vs. build forward. Control closes the topic by summarizing it back — accurate, and exactly what its design asks for. The simple prompt uses the same turn to push the conversation forward, which is how it reached the "3–5% of SKUs" figure below. Both are valid moves; the second one keeps the dialogue moving and gains ground.

The messy-data problem · how bad is it, exactly?

Control · today's prompt

"We have some data, but I wouldn't call it clean or consistently structured… the 'why' is often buried in comments or inconsistent codes… we're not instrumented the way an engineering team would expect."

Simple prompt

"Roughly, I'd say it shows up in the low single digits of SKUs in any given week — maybe 3–5% have some kind of ingestion or tagging issue that requires human intervention. It doesn't sound huge, but at our volume it's constant noise."

Specifics vs. generalities. Same underlying problem — unreliable asset data. Control captured the shape of it well ("it's messy, the truth lives in comments"). The simple prompt got a quantified failure rate and, right after, a dated rollback incident — the vivid, concrete detail a credible report is built from.

What's holding this up · the most candid moment in each

Control · today's prompt

"The uncomfortable truth is that the hardest part isn't generating pixels or words — it's getting the organization to agree on what 'good' is and then stick to it." (a truth about the org, not about himself)

Simple prompt

"I'm probably the bottleneck in how quickly we move from 'pilot' to 'standard'… on AI the blast radius feels bigger than a normal tooling change. So I tend to push for discipline and proof, and I'm aware that can read as slower than what leadership wants."

The most important difference. Both reached a candid moment. Control's pointed outward — a sharp, honest truth about the organization. The simple prompt asked the blunt personal question and got Matt to turn the lens on himself: "I'm probably the bottleneck." For a leadership read, the admission about the person is the gold — and only one interview went there.

How the conversation landed · last turn of each transcript

Simple prompt

Why the simpler prompt produced the better interview

  • It felt like a conversation, not a questionnaire.

    The simple prompt opened by naming the human tension and asking an open question, then let each answer steer the next one. The 16-module is built to work a coverage plan and reflect each answer back before advancing — competent and thorough, but it reads as a well-run interview protocol rather than a dialogue. For a Mirror read, how the conversation feels shapes how much the person gives you.

  • It kept its momentum — questions built on answers.

    Watch the chain: pipeline walkthrough → "which break hurts most?" → "what's the most common error, and how often?" → "% of SKUs?" → a dated rollback story. Each turn earns the next. The control's cadence — ask, then summarize back — is steady and accurate, but it resets the energy at every step instead of compounding it.

  • It got him to talk about himself, not just his org.

    This is the decisive difference. The simple prompt asked the blunt personal question and got "I'm probably the bottleneck… I'm aware that can read as slower than what leadership wants" — and ended on a values question about who he wants to be remembered as. The control surfaced genuinely sharp truths, but they pointed at the organization and the system, not the man. A leadership read lives on the personal layer.

  • Its specifics were sharper and more quotable.

    Real numbers and vivid incidents — retouch "10–15 minutes down to 5–8," tagging issues on "3–5% of SKUs," the copywriter who pasted PIM notes into personal ChatGPT, the lifestyle image rolled back after it went live. The control earned strong strategic material too (the 10–15% vs 25–40% expectation gap is excellent), but the simple prompt's detail is the kind that makes a report feel true rather than summarized.

Honest caveats & the fine print

This is a quality judgment, not a quality verdict. It's one persona, one run each, judged by reading two transcripts. It's directional and it's persuasive — but it isn't a generalized result yet.

A machine-signal artifact affects both arms equally. Each turn, the interviewer is supposed to emit small hidden "control signals" (how far along it is, what topic, whether it's done) that the real platform uses to drive flow. This test ran on gpt-5.2 through a gateway, outside our production platform, and both prompts dropped those signals on most turns. That's a plumbing artifact of where we ran the test, not a sign of either prompt's conversation quality — and our production platform enforces the structure far more strictly. It needs verifying on the real platform, but it doesn't change the qualitative read either way.

One minor glitch worth noting: in the control run the interviewer asked the same enforcement question twice in a row, nearly verbatim. A small hiccup, not a pattern — flagged here for honesty, not as part of the verdict.

Recommended next step

Run the same head-to-head across 3–4 more interviewee personalities — a guarded skeptic, a chatty over-sharer, a junior IC — to see whether the warmer prompt keeps drawing people out, or whether the structured approach earns its keep with harder subjects. If the conversational edge holds, the simpler prompt is a strong candidate to replace today's system. Verify the control-signal behavior on the production platform in parallel.
