What this is about

Mirror is an AI-led interview. A person sits down with an AI interviewer that asks them a series of questions about their work and how they lead. From that conversation, Mirror writes a personal leadership "read" of them — a short Insights Snapshot and a longer personal Mirror Report.

The quality of that read depends entirely on the quality of the interview. A flat, mechanical conversation produces a thin, generic report; a natural one that genuinely draws the person out produces a richer, truer one.

What steers the interviewer is a "system prompt" — a block of written instructions, invisible to the person being interviewed, that tells the AI how to behave. Our current prompt is large and intricate. The question we wanted to answer: could a much simpler, warmer prompt do the job as well, or better? So we ran a head-to-head test.

What we compared

Two sets of instructions for the same AI interviewer. Nothing else about the test changed between them.

Option A · what we use today

The elaborate prompt

~27,000 characters of structured orchestration

Internally we call it the "16-module" prompt. It lays out distinct phases, a coverage checklist of topics to hit, and strict rules for how to ask. Thorough and disciplined by design — but it reads more like a well-run questionnaire than a conversation.

Option B · the challenger

The simple, warm prompt

~5,800 characters — roughly one fifth the size

A single short instruction written in a warm, conversational tone. No phase map, no checklist — just a clear brief to be curious, ask one good question at a time, and follow the person. The experiment we wanted to validate.

How we tested it

We can't run a test like this on a different live human each time — so we simulated it with AI, holding everything fixed except the one thing we wanted to test.

The interviewer

One AI, run twice — once with each prompt. This is the only thing that changed.

↔

The interviewee

A second AI playing the same pretend person both times. Identical in every run.

↓

The judge

A third AI — from a different company (Anthropic's Claude) — read both transcripts and scored them, to avoid any home-team bias.

  1. The pretend interviewee was held constant. Both times, the interviewee was "Matt, Head of Creative Operations at Wayfair" — a calm VP two levels below the CEO who runs a ~90-person content-production org, and who is quietly anxious about what AI means for his team and his own relevance. Same person, same backstory, both runs.
  2. Only the interviewer's prompt changed. Run 1 used the elaborate prompt; run 2 used the simple prompt. Everything else was identical, so any difference in the conversation traces back to the prompt.
  3. A different AI company judged the result. Using a model from another vendor to grade the transcripts keeps us from marking our own homework.

The interviewer and interviewee were both OpenAI's gpt-5.2. The judge was Anthropic's Claude. We judged the conversations qualitatively — by reading them — on five things: how natural they felt, how well they flowed, how much rapport and candor they built, how rich the material they elicited was, and how ready that material was to become a good report. Those five matter because a more natural conversation that genuinely draws a person out is what yields a richer, truer leadership read.

What we found

Both interviewers were competent and both covered the right ground — this is not a story about one breaking down. The simpler prompt won on the quality of the conversation: it read more like a real dialogue, kept its momentum, drew out sharper specifics, and got the person to talk about himself, not just his organization. Scored 1–5, higher is better.

1. How each interview opened The very first thing each interviewer said, before any answers

Elaborate prompt · what we use today

Interviewer opened with

"Hey Matt — good to have you here. We've got about 30–45 minutes… Before I dig in, here's what I'm picking up: Your day job: you run the content factory… Your 'team' is a machine: a ~90-person mixed-discipline org… The AI ask is political now: leadership wants your point of view soon… Does that feel right? You can say 'yes,' or give me as much feedback as you want."

Simple prompt

Interviewer opened with

"Matt — thanks for making the time. Creative ops is one of those worlds where 'AI' isn't abstract: it hits the throughput math, the on-brand guardrails, and the human pride of craft all at once, and suddenly you're responsible for all three. Where are your work hours actually going right now — what are the 2–3 things that eat most of your week?"

More natural. The current prompt opened with a tidy summary of what it already knew and asked Matt to confirm it — which invites a yes/no. The simple prompt named the human tension ("the human pride of craft," "responsible for all three") and then handed him an open question — which invites him to actually talk.

2. Probing a failed AI tool experiment Both asked about the same real pilot: using an Adobe tool called Firefly to auto-fill image backgrounds and speed up photo retouching

Elaborate prompt · what we use today

It asked

"In those 'pockets of testing,' what AI use cases are actually in production right now (even if small), and which ones have been tried and dropped?"

And got

"The one that's closest to 'real' is Firefly generative fill in Photoshop for certain background extensions or cleanup… It does save minutes, but it also goes off-brand or introduces weird artifacts often enough that we've kept it very scoped… anything that creates even a 10% rework rate at our volumes is basically a non-starter."

Simple prompt

It asked

"In that Firefly pilot, what was the actual before/after on time per asset and the QA loop — how many minutes saved in retouch, and how many minutes added back in review/escalation?"

And got

"Directionally the retouch step might drop from, say, 10–15 minutes down to 5–8 when it's a straightforward extension… the QA loop can go from essentially 'quick check' to 10+ minutes of scrutiny… So on the good assets you net a few minutes, and on the bad ones you lose a lot more than you saved."

Richer answer. Both heard about the same tool. The current prompt got a clear qualitative read — "saves minutes," but "weird artifacts." The simple prompt pressed one level further and surfaced the hidden trap with real numbers: the time the tool saves comes straight back in extra checking. Same topic, far more usable material.

3. What each interviewer did right after a long answer Right after Matt walked through how content moves through his pipeline — the choice between summarizing it back versus digging deeper

Elaborate prompt · what we use today

It reflected the answer back

"So the pain isn't 'creative magic' — it's inputs, handoffs, and rework loops that eat the gains."

…then moved to its next planned topic.

Simple prompt

It dug one level deeper

"On the metadata/ingestion break, what's the most common concrete error — wrong filename, missing attribute, wrong channel spec, version confusion — and how often does it happen (rough % of SKUs)?"

…and got: "maybe 3–5% have some kind of ingestion or tagging issue."

Kept the momentum. The current prompt closes the topic by summarizing it back — accurate, and exactly what its design tells it to do. The simple prompt used the same turn to push forward, which is how it reached the concrete "3–5% of SKUs" figure. Both are valid moves; the second one compounds instead of resetting.

4. How bad is the data, exactly? Both explored whether Matt's team has clean enough records to make their workflow smarter with AI

Elaborate prompt · what we use today

It asked

"What's your data situation for making routing or QA smarter — do you have clean tags on what got rejected, why, how long it sat in each stage, which vendors/studios performed well, etc.?"

And got

"We have some data, but I wouldn't call it clean or consistently structured… the 'why' is often buried in comments or inconsistent codes… we're not instrumented the way an engineering team would expect."

Simple prompt

It asked

"On the metadata/ingestion break, what's the most common concrete error… and how often does it happen (rough % of SKUs)?"

And got

"Roughly, I'd say it shows up in the low single digits of SKUs in any given week — maybe 3–5% have some kind of ingestion or tagging issue that requires human intervention. It doesn't sound huge, but at our volume it's constant noise…"

Specifics, not generalities. Same underlying problem — unreliable asset data. The current prompt captured the shape of it well ("it's messy, the truth lives in comments"). The simple prompt got a quantified failure rate, and right after it, a dated rollback story — the concrete detail a credible report is built from.

5. What's really holding this up — and is it him? The most candid moment each interview reached. The difference here is the whole point.

Elaborate prompt · what we use today

It asked

"What have I not asked that you think is the real crux — one uncomfortable truth about your factory, your stakeholders, or AI — that you want reflected before you put your name on a plan?"

And got (a truth about the org)

"The uncomfortable truth is that the hardest part isn't generating pixels or words — it's getting the organization to agree on what 'good' is and then stick to it…"

Simple prompt

It asked

"If you're brutally honest, what's the biggest bottleneck you personally are right now in all of this?"

And got (a truth about himself)

"I'm probably the bottleneck in how quickly we move from 'pilot' to 'standard,' because I default to wanting the process and controls clear before I scale… on AI the blast radius feels bigger than a normal tooling change… I'm aware that can read as slower than what leadership wants."

Drew him out — about himself. Both reached a candid moment, but they point in opposite directions. The current prompt got a sharp, honest truth about the organization. The simple prompt asked the blunt personal question and got Matt to turn the lens on himself: "I'm probably the bottleneck." For a personal leadership read, that admission is the gold — and only one interview reached it.

Where the current prompt was genuinely good

This isn't a one-sided result. The elaborate prompt earned real strengths — and on a couple of moments it was the better interviewer. Credit where it's due:

  • It earned the single sharpest line in either interview.

    Pushed for the "uncomfortable truth," the current prompt drew out a strategic insight neither prompt beat:

    "…the hardest part isn't generating pixels or words — it's getting the organization to agree on what 'good' is and then stick to it."

  • It surfaced the leadership-vs-operator expectation gap.

    Early on, it pinned down a tension that goes to the core of Matt's situation: what he thinks is realistically achievable versus what leadership is demanding. In his words, "if we can take even 10–15% out of turnaround… that's meaningful in my world; leadership tends to talk like 25–40% cost-out, and I'm not convinced that's realistic." That gap is exactly the kind of strategic material a report needs — and the structured prompt's coverage discipline is what got there.

  • Thoroughness is a real feature, not just a cost.

    The elaborate prompt's habit of reflecting each answer back and working a coverage checklist makes it dependable: it is unlikely to miss a topic it was told to hit. For some uses — a structured audit, a guarded subject who needs the conversation steered — that discipline may be worth more than warmth. This test doesn't settle that; it only tested one kind of person.

What to keep in mind

This is one pretend interviewee, run once each. The result is directional and persuasive — but it is not proof. A single simulated person can't tell us how the two prompts behave with a guarded skeptic, a chatty over-sharer, or a junior employee. Treat this as a strong signal pointing to the next test, not a final verdict.

The models used. The AI interviewer and the AI interviewee were both OpenAI's gpt-5.2. The judge was Anthropic's Claude — deliberately a different company, so the grading wasn't home-team marking its own work.

A minor setup artifact, not a quality issue, affected both prompts. Each turn, the interviewer is meant to emit small hidden "machine signals" — how far along it is, what topic it's on, whether it's done — that the real Mirror platform uses to drive the flow. This test ran off the production platform (through a gateway), and on most turns both prompts dropped those signals. That's an artifact of where we ran the test, not a reflection of either prompt's conversation quality, and it doesn't change the read. We'll confirm the signals behave on the real platform, where the structure is enforced far more strictly.

Recommendation & next step

The simple, warm prompt is a strong candidate to replace today's elaborate one — but on a sample of one we shouldn't switch yet. Two steps to get to a real decision:

  1. Run the same head-to-head across more interviewee types — a guarded skeptic, a chatty over-sharer, a junior individual contributor — to see whether the warmer prompt keeps drawing people out, or whether the structured prompt earns its keep with harder subjects.
  2. Verify on the production platform — re-run on the real Mirror platform to confirm the hidden machine signals behave as intended once the structure is enforced, ruling out the setup artifact above.
