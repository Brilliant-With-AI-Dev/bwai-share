Same moment, two interviewers

Both interviewers reached the same topics with the same person. Here are six moments side by side — Control (today's prompt) on the left, the simple prompt on the right, in their own words. The contrast is the whole argument.

The opening line · first thing each interviewer said

Control · today's prompt

"Before I dig in, here's what I'm picking up: Your day job: you run the content factory… Your 'team' is a machine: a ~90-person mixed-discipline org… The AI ask is political now… Does that feel right?"

Simple prompt

"Creative ops is one of those worlds where 'AI' isn't abstract: it hits the throughput math, the on-brand guardrails, and the human pride of craft all at once, and suddenly you're responsible for all three. Where are your work hours actually going right now?"

Built rapport. Control read a briefing back at the person and asked them to grade it. The simple prompt named the quiet anxiety — pride of craft, "responsible for all three" — then opened the floor. One sounds like a status report; the other sounds like a conversation.

The Firefly retouch pilot · "what did it actually save?"

Control · today's prompt

"It does save minutes, but it also goes off-brand or introduces weird artifacts often enough that we've kept it very scoped… anything that creates even a 10% rework rate at our volumes is basically a non-starter."

Simple prompt

"Directionally the retouch step might drop from, say, 10–15 minutes down to 5–8 when it's a straightforward extension… The problem is the QA loop can go from essentially 'quick check' to 10+ minutes of scrutiny… on the good assets you net a few minutes, and on the bad ones you lose a lot more than you saved."

Extracted a real number. Both heard about the same pilot. Control left it at "saves minutes" / "weird artifacts." The simple prompt asked the follow-up that pinned the actual before / after — and surfaced the hidden trap: the time saved comes right back in QA.

After the pipeline walkthrough · what the interviewer did next

Control · today's prompt

[restated the answer back] "So the pain isn't 'creative magic' — it's inputs, handoffs, and rework loops that eat the gains."

Simple prompt

[drilled one level deeper] "On the metadata/ingestion break, what's the most common concrete error — wrong filename, missing attribute, wrong channel spec, version confusion — and how often does it happen (rough % of SKUs)?"

Avoided parroting. Control spent its turn summarizing what it had just been told — pleasant, but it adds nothing. The simple prompt spent the same turn mining for a number, which is how it got the "3–5% of SKUs" figure below.

The messy-data problem · how bad is it, exactly?

Control · today's prompt

"We have some data, but I wouldn't call it clean or consistently structured… the 'why' is often buried in comments or inconsistent codes… we're not instrumented the way an engineering team would expect."

Simple prompt

"Roughly, I'd say it shows up in the low single digits of SKUs in any given week — maybe 3–5% have some kind of ingestion or tagging issue that requires human intervention. It doesn't sound huge, but at our volume it's constant noise."

Went one level deeper. Same underlying problem — unreliable asset data. Control got a qualitative "it's messy." The simple prompt got a quantified failure rate and a dated rollback incident right after it — the kind of detail a credible report is built from.

What's holding this up · the most candid moment in each

Control · today's prompt

"The uncomfortable truth is that the hardest part isn't generating pixels or words — it's getting the organization to agree on what 'good' is and then stick to it." (a truth about the org, not about himself)

Simple prompt

"I'm probably the bottleneck in how quickly we move from 'pilot' to 'standard'… on AI the blast radius feels bigger than a normal tooling change. So I tend to push for discipline and proof, and I'm aware that can read as slower than what leadership wants."

Surfaced the hidden fear. Control's "knife on the table" pointed outward — at the organization. The simple prompt asked Matt the blunt personal question — "what's the biggest bottleneck you personally are right now in all of this?" — and got him to name himself. That admission is the heart of a leadership read.

How the interview ended · final turn of each transcript

Control · today's prompt

Still asking new questions when the 40-turn safety cap cut it off — never signalled "done": "What's the first concrete milestone you'd put on the calendar that forces cross-functional alignment…"

Simple prompt

Wound down cleanly and marked itself complete: "Thanks — that's clear. …would you rather be remembered for hitting the cost-out number even if it annoyed creatives, or for protecting craft even if it missed the savings target — and why?"

Knew when to stop. Control never wound down — it ran into the safety cap mid-question. The simple prompt landed on a clean capstone and flagged itself finished. Left alone, one knows the interview is over; the other doesn't.

Why it won — in business terms

  • Better rapport → people open up → a richer report.

    The warm prompt opened like a real conversation and drew out the interviewee's hidden anxiety — he admitted the story leadership tells about AI "has gotten ahead of the reality," and named himself as the bottleneck. The 16-module opened with a cold bullet-point briefing and left that personal fear unspoken.

  • More concrete answers → a sharper, more credible deliverable.

    The warm prompt pulled out real numbers — retouch time dropping "10–15 minutes down to 5–8," tagging issues on "3–5% of SKUs." The 16-module stayed vague ("about a third drifted"). Specifics are what make a leadership read believable.

  • More disciplined → less wasted, repetitive talk.

    The 16-module kept restating the person's answers back at them and even asked the same question twice. The warm prompt didn't.

  • It actually finishes.

    The warm prompt completed in about 36 questions and signaled it was done. The 16-module never wound down and ran into the 40-turn safety cap — meaning, left to itself, it doesn't know when to stop.

  • Cheaper to own.

    One-fifth the size means far less to maintain, tune, and trust. A shorter prompt is easier to reason about and harder to break.

The one honest caveat

Every turn, the interviewer is supposed to emit small machine "control signals" — how far along it is, what topic it's on, whether it's done — that the system uses to drive the flow. In this test, run on OpenAI's gpt-5.2 outside our production platform, both prompts frequently dropped those signals mid-interview (roughly two-thirds of turns).

This is most likely a pessimistic result: our real platform enforces that structure far more strictly, so production would probably hold up better. But that is unverified.

Bottom line: the simpler prompt is the clear winner on interview quality. Before adopting it, we should (1) confirm the win holds across more interviewee types, and (2) verify the control signals behave on the real platform.

Recommended next step

Run the same head-to-head across 3–4 more interviewee personalities to confirm the win generalizes, and verify control-signal behavior on the production platform. If both hold, the simpler prompt is a strong candidate to replace today's system.
