Three moves. Four weeks. None of them require permission from anyone but you.

1
Name the owner

By Friday, one person owns AI for marketing — written into their goals, not run as a side project.

Why this, why now You called twenty-one people "waiting for permission you have not given them." An owner is how the permission stops being yours to withhold.

2
Run one workflow in public

Pick the campaign-brief workflow. Run it end-to-end where the team can watch it beat the old way.

Why this, why now Your letter said the team would believe it when they "watch it beat the old way," not when they're told. One public win does what a memo can't.

3
Decide which job is yours

The best marketer at Northwind, or the person who makes twelve marketers better. Pick — and say it out loud.

Why this, why now You wrote that "the efficiency target will make that choice for you if you don't." Deciding now is how you keep the choice yours.

Act 3 · Check-intwo weeks in — opened from one email

A check-in that remembers your plan — not a reminder.

Two or three of these arrive across the thirty days. Each one opens an experience that already knows your current Action Plan and asks the only questions that matter: what moved, what's blocked, what's changed. Answering can mint an annotated plan or a whole new version — and every nudge can answer "why am I seeing this," so it never feels like a generic reminder. Progress is something you tell it, never something it scrapes or scores.

plan-aware, never generic · what moved is yours to tell · updates the plan, not a score

Act 3 · Time to returnthe signal lives outside the chat

When enough has changed, the product says so — out loud, outside the chat.

The home surface always shows where everything stands — your current Mirror, your current plan, and the assistant. And when enough has accumulated — time passed, moves done, what the check-ins heard — Return to Mirror lights up as a ready ceremony with a plain-language why now. The ritual gets its own threshold instead of drowning in chat noise: a door that opens on purpose, not a line that scrolls past.
