Turn 1 · the opening

Real Mirror narrative

QWhat are you personally accountable for right now — the 2–3 outcomes that, if they don't move, you “lose the year”? Think make-or-break, not the nice-to-haves from the strategy deck.

AThree things: on-time delivery in my region, cost-to-ship staying within plan, and weekly capacity accuracy. If those slip for a couple weeks, I'm in escalation mode and it becomes the story of the quarter.

Alt-Mirror scored

QTo start, what's your current role and the kind of work you do week-to-week — team, industry, and your main responsibilities?

ASenior Operations Manager at Northwind Logistics, running regional fulfilment across a few sites. Capacity planning, service-level performance, carrier exception triage, plus weekly forecasts and KPI rollups.

Turn 2 · chasing the signal

Real Mirror narrative

QWhen your director starts escalating, what do they actually blame first? I'm not asking what's fair; I'm asking where the heat tends to land in the real post-mortem.

AMy planning process first — “why didn't we see it coming.” Carrier performance second, demand volatility only as a fallback once I've shown I had a plan.

Alt-Mirror scored

QWhere are you using AI in that weekly cadence right now — name the tool(s) and walk me through one specific recent task: what you input, what you got out, how you used it.

AClaude in the chat window, manual. Last Tuesday I pasted ~200 lines of carrier exceptions, had it bucket them and draft a carrier email; I checked a few rows and sent it.

Turn 5 · the high-signal probe

Real Mirror narrative

QWhat's the most valuable thing you've used AI for this month — the one use you'd be annoyed to lose tomorrow? Keep it concrete.

ACarrier-exception summaries, hands down. A clean narrative in 15–20 minutes instead of an hour of manual pivoting — it plugs straight into my on-time and cost story.

Alt-Mirror scored

QTell me about one specific time (give the date) when Claude's output was wrong or risky — what it recommended, how you caught it, what you changed before acting.

AWeek of April 15th it invented a throughput rate I never gave it. The number didn't match our dashboard, so I removed it, re-ran with real figures, and added “do not infer rates.”

Turn 9 · reading the room

Real Mirror narrative

QWhat's the AI vibe across your 8-person team — who's quietly good at it, who's allergic, and what's the dominant emotion? Name archetypes if that's easier.

AIndifferent-to-curious; nobody uses it daily but me. My analysts are the likeliest to get good but wary of sending a wrong number; dispatchers are allergic, supervisors overwhelmed.

Alt-Mirror scored

QFor your May 9 rollup note about the 93.4% vs 95.1% discrepancy, did you use AI to draft it? If yes, paste the exact prompt and what you edited before sending.

AYes. Prompt asked for a 3–5 sentence explanation, the number we're using and why, the fix and owner. I cut a line blaming the carrier and added the ~80-shipment impact.

Notice: Mirror pushes toward identity and politics (“what do they actually blame”); Alt-Mirror pushes toward verifiable evidence (“give the date, paste the prompt”). Same person, two different things surfaced.

Real Mirror — the letter ~2,500 words · no scores

“A Letter to Dana, Who Is Being Judged for Weather”

“This is not a report. This is a mirror. Some of what you see will be uncomfortable. Read it anyway.”

The truth: You're not failing at operations — you're failing at courtroom-speed proof, and that's why “two bad weeks” turns political.

Do this now: Build a weekly “receipts packet” that reconciles your three systems in under 30 minutes.

Do this next: Turn carrier exceptions into a repeatable scoreboard so escalations become a pattern conversation, not a blame conversation.

Before it's too late: Stop being the human integration layer — that role becomes a permanent escalation sponge.

  • The Lie

    Three comfortable stories — “leadership just wants us to explore it safely,” “the dashboard is truth,” “AI isn't worth it because it hallucinates” — and why each one is quietly costing her.

  • The Blind Spots

    “You think you're evaluated on outcomes; you're evaluated on foresight.” “You're becoming the irreplaceable bottleneck you'll later resent.”

  • What You Have That Most Don't

    She can see patterns and write the narrative leadership reads — the exact interface between messy execution and executive action.

  • The Compass Heading

    Concrete moves at three horizons: this week, this quarter, this year — receipts packet, exception taxonomy, volatility-aware planning.

Where this run fell short

No high-value opportunity or confirmed blind spot surfaced, and the personalization profile came back mostly empty (“ask user”). The scoring spine is real; the narrative and profile layers still need wiring.

What's working

Both ran end-to-end on the live backend, interviewing the same person in parallel. Real Mirror's letter is genuinely strong — identity-level, motivating, ready. Alt-Mirror's scoring engine works live, deriving sensible skill levels from counted evidence.

The honest gap

Today, Real Mirror's letter is the richer human artifact. Alt-Mirror's report and profile are thin — it scores skills well but doesn't yet surface opportunities, blind spots, or a filled-in profile. Those are wiring gaps, not engine failures.

The decision in front of us

The two engines are almost the same machine. The real choice is the surface:

A · keep them distinct  Run both as foils — feel the scored report against the narrative letter. (current path)

B · converge  Keep Alt-Mirror's scoring as the hidden engine and render a Mirror-style letter on top. Scores power the machine; the human gets the letter.
